# Probate Grand Slam Offer v1

## 0. Offer-first decision

This is the core seller offer Ares should build around before writing ads, emails, direct mail, SMS, calls, landing pages, or close scripts.

The seller is not buying “a cash offer.” The seller is trying to get relief, clarity, and optionality around an inherited property that may be turning into a family project.

## 1. Recommended offer name

# The Inherited Property Relief Plan

### Why this name is stronger than “cash offer”

- It sells the outcome: relief from the inherited-property burden.
- It does not sound like a generic investor commodity.
- It gives room to include more than one path: keep, sell, rent, wait, or simply understand whether an as-is option exists.
- It is emotionally safer than “probate sale” because it does not force the seller to identify as a motivated seller.

## 2. One-line offer

A simple way for Texas families to find out whether an inherited property can be sold as-is — without repairing it, cleaning it out, listing it, coordinating showings, or solving every title, tax, heirship, or paperwork question before the first conversation.

## 3. Plain-English seller-facing version

If your family inherited a property and it is starting to feel like one more project, I can review it as-is and tell you whether a practical buyer option is even worth discussing.

You do not need to fix it, clean it out, list it, stage it, host showings, get every document perfect, or have every family member fully aligned before asking the question.

If keeping it is the plan, no problem. If selling as-is would help, we can talk through what that might look like.

## 4. Core market thesis

Probate sellers are not mainly looking for a buyer. They are looking for a way to make the property stop being a vague, stressful, unfinished responsibility.

The pain is not only “I need money.” The pain is:

- “I do not know what to do with this house.”
- “I do not want this to become another family fight.”
- “I do not have time to clean this out.”
- “I do not want to spend money fixing a house I may not keep.”
- “I do not know if the paperwork is ready.”
- “I do not want to be embarrassed by the condition.”
- “I do not want to deal with showings.”
- “I need a simple next step that does not make me commit to everything right now.”

That means the offer must sell clarity, low effort, and optionality before it sells the transaction.

## 5. Hormozi value equation

Perceived Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort/Sacrifice)

### Dream outcome

The seller wants:

- to know whether the inherited property has a realistic as-is path
- to get the property off their mental plate if they do not want it
- to avoid making repairs on a house they may sell anyway
- to avoid cleaning out years of belongings before knowing the path
- to avoid becoming a probate/title/tax expert just to ask a basic question
- to keep peace with family members where possible
- to feel like they are making a responsible decision, not being pressured

### Perceived likelihood boosters

Ares/Martin can increase belief by making the process specific:

1. **Local inherited-property focus**
   - This is for inherited/probate-style property situations, not a generic retail listing pitch.

2. **As-is review before seller homework**
   - The first step is a review of the property situation as it is, not a checklist of repairs the family must complete.

3. **Friction-aware buyer conversation**
   - Repairs, cleanout, taxes, title questions, vacant/occupied condition, distance, and multiple-heir friction are treated as context.

4. **Clear fit / no-fit answer**
   - The seller gets a useful read: worth discussing, not a fit, or needs another practical next step.

5. **No legal/tax overclaiming**
   - Trust increases when the offer admits boundaries instead of pretending every issue can be magically solved.

### Time-delay reducers

- Start with a short call, text, or property review.
- No listing appointment required to ask the first question.
- No repairs before the first conversation.
- No cleanout before the first conversation.
- No showings before the seller knows whether as-is is relevant.
- No need to gather every document before asking whether a buyer option may exist.

### Effort / sacrifice reducers

- No cleaning out the house first.
- No coordinating contractors first.
- No staging or open houses.
- No MLS prep.
- No perfect family consensus before asking the initial question.
- No pressure if the family decides to keep the property.
- No shame around the current condition.

## 6. Offer code / Rosetta Stone

The code we should infuse everywhere:

### Repeated “without” code

- without fixing it first
- without cleaning it out first
- without paying contractors before you know the path
- without listing it publicly
- without open houses or showings
- without getting every document perfect first
- without every heir having the whole plan figured out before the first conversation
- without pretending the house is retail-ready

### Time / speed code

Probate sellers are often busy and emotionally drained. They need the first step to feel light:

- “quick read”
- “simple next step”
- “worth discussing or not”
- “before you spend time or money”
- “start with the property as it sits”

### Flexibility / real-life edge-case code

The offer must work when life is messy:

- family members disagree
- someone lives out of town
- the house is full of belongings
- repairs are obvious
- taxes may be due
- title or heirship is unclear
- nobody wants to host showings
- one person is carrying the responsibility
- the seller is not sure they are even the right person

### Good-enough permission code

The seller does not need to have a perfect situation to start:

- the house does not have to be cleaned
- paperwork does not have to be perfect
- the family decision does not have to be final
- the seller does not have to know whether they want to sell yet
- the first conversation can simply create clarity

## 7. Unique mechanism

# The As-Is Inherited Property Review

### Mechanism explanation

Instead of asking the family to prepare the property for the normal sale path first, Martin reviews the inherited-property situation as it is:

- property condition
- cleanout burden
- repair burden
- seller goal
- family decision stage
- known title/tax/heirship friction, without giving legal or tax advice
- whether an as-is buyer option is even worth discussing

The mechanism is not “we buy houses.”

The mechanism is:

> Start with a practical as-is review so the family can decide whether selling is worth discussing before they spend time, money, or emotional energy preparing the property for a path they may not want.

## 8. Offer stack

The seller gets these value pieces:

### 1. As-Is Property Review

A practical review of the inherited property as it currently sits — condition, location, occupancy, repairs, cleanout, and general sale friction.

### 2. No-Prep First Conversation

The family can ask the question before repairs, cleanout, showings, staging, listing prep, or perfect paperwork.

### 3. Inherited-Property Friction Map

A plain-English discussion of what appears to be making the decision harder:

- repairs
- cleanout
- taxes
- title questions
- multiple heirs
- distance
- vacant / occupied property
- timing pressure

This is not legal or tax advice. It is a practical seller conversation.

### 4. Keep / Sell / Wait Clarity

The seller does not have to pretend they are ready to sell. The first useful outcome may be simply knowing:

- selling as-is is worth discussing
- keeping it makes more sense right now
- waiting may be reasonable
- another family member is the better contact
- more information is needed before any serious decision

### 5. Practical Buyer Option If It Fits

If the property and situation fit, Martin can discuss an as-is buyer option.

### 6. Clean Exit If It Does Not Fit

If it is not relevant, the seller can say so and Martin closes his notes.

## 9. Risk reversal / de-pressure

Use this language:

- “If keeping it is the plan, no problem.”
- “If I am not the right fit, I will tell you.”
- “You do not need to repair or clean it out before we talk.”
- “You do not need every document perfect before asking the first question.”
- “If I have the wrong person, tell me and I will close my notes.”
- “This is simply to see whether an as-is option is worth discussing.”

Avoid this language:

- risk-free
- guaranteed offer
- guaranteed close
- guaranteed title cure
- guaranteed tax solution
- we handle probate
- we solve legal problems
- we buy every property

## 10. Copy hinge

If the inherited property is becoming one more project — repairs, cleanup, taxes, title questions, or deciding what everyone in the family wants to do — this is simply to give you a useful read on whether an as-is option is worth discussing.

You do not have to fix it, clean it out, list it, or have every document perfect before asking. In a few minutes, you should be able to tell whether this is relevant or whether I should close my notes and leave you alone.

## 12. Segment-specific offer versions

### HOT — friction is already visible

Offer angle:

A practical as-is path for inherited properties that are already turning into a repair, cleanout, tax, title, or family-decision project.

Best promise:

Find out whether an as-is option is worth discussing before spending more time or money trying to make the house retail-ready.

Best CTA:

“I can give you a quick read on whether an as-is option may make sense before the family puts more work into the property.”

### WARM — decision is forming

Offer angle:

A low-pressure way to understand whether selling as-is belongs on the family’s list of options.

Best promise:

Get clarity without committing to a sale, listing the house, or preparing it for showings.

Best CTA:

“I can show you the simple version of what I look for so you can decide whether it is relevant.”

### COLD — low awareness / wrong-person risk

Offer angle:

A wrong-person-safe note about an inherited property, with an easy way to opt out.

Best promise:

If this is not relevant, the seller can simply say so and be left alone.

Best CTA:

“If I have the wrong person, just reply ‘wrong person’ and I will close my notes.”

## 13. Offer ladder

### Step 1 — attention offer

A quick read on whether an as-is option is even worth discussing.

### Step 2 — conversation offer

A no-prep inherited-property review: condition, cleanout, repairs, seller goal, and whether as-is makes sense.

### Step 3 — transaction offer

If it fits, a practical as-is buyer option with no repairs, cleanout, showings, or retail prep before the conversation.

### Step 4 — close support

A simple next-step path with clear boundaries, no legal/tax advice claims, and no guarantee of purchase/closing/title cure.

## 14. What this offer should replace

Do not lead with:

- “I buy houses cash.”
- “Get a cash offer.”
- “We buy probate houses.”
- “Sell your house fast.”
- “Avoid foreclosure/probate problems.”

Lead with:

- “Find out whether an as-is option is worth discussing.”
- “Before you repair, clean out, list, or solve every paperwork issue first.”
- “If keeping it is the plan, no problem.”
- “If I have the wrong person, I will close my notes.”

## 16. Final current offer

# The Inherited Property Relief Plan

A simple, no-prep way to find out whether an inherited property has a practical as-is path — before the family repairs it, cleans it out, lists it, hosts showings, gets every document perfect, or solves every title/tax/heirship question first.

The first step is not a sales pitch. It is a quick as-is review so the family can decide whether selling is worth discussing, keeping it makes more sense, or Martin should close his notes and leave them alone.
