# Real Estate Investor Multi-Channel Marketing Playbook

**Focus:** Cold email, direct mail, SMS, and multi-channel campaigns for sourcing distressed/off-market real estate at a discount.

**Primary channels:** Cold email and direct mail  
**Secondary channel:** SMS, only where consent/compliance allows

---

## Executive Take

For distressed/off-market real estate, **cold email is your speed and testing channel**; **direct mail is your trust and persistence channel**; **SMS is not your cold-prospecting channel unless you have documented consent**. Cold texting random homeowners is a lawsuit magnet, not a growth strategy.

Also: “sourcing distressed real estate at a massive discount” is your internal acquisition objective, not your seller-facing message. Sellers do not want to hear that you are hunting their pain. The external message is:

> **Simple as-is sale, no repairs, no agents, flexible closing, no pressure, transparent offer.**

The strongest system is not “blast everyone.” It is:

```text
Property-list segmentation
→ direct mail drop
→ timed cold email
→ inbound call/text capture
→ live qualification
→ offer
→ nurture
→ reactivation
```

# 1. Compliance and Risk Rules

## Cold Email

Cold email is not automatically illegal in the U.S., but it must comply with CAN-SPAM. The FTC says CAN-SPAM applies to all commercial messages and explicitly says there is no business-to-business exception. You need accurate header/from information, non-deceptive subject lines, an opt-out mechanism, and a valid physical postal address. The FTC also lists penalties up to $53,088 per separate violating email.

Source: [FTC CAN-SPAM Compliance Guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business)

For deliverability, Gmail and Yahoo are effectively the gatekeepers. Gmail requires SPF or DKIM for all senders, TLS, valid DNS, and spam rates below 0.3%. Gmail bulk senders have additional requirements starting at 5,000+ Gmail messages per day. Yahoo similarly requires authentication, low complaint rates under 0.3%, and for bulk senders requires SPF, DKIM, DMARC, one-click/list unsubscribe, and honoring unsubscribes within two days.

Sources:

- [Google Email Sender Guidelines](https://support.google.com/a/answer/81126?hl=en)
- [Yahoo Sender Best Practices](https://senders.yahooinc.com/best-practices/)

**Operator rule:** every cold email must include a clean opt-out line. Do not make people hunt for it. If they reply “remove me,” suppress them across email, mail, calls, and SMS.

## SMS

SMS is powerful, but cold SMS to homeowners is dangerous. TCPA and carrier rules require clear consent for recurring marketing texts. Twilio’s U.S. compliance guidance says simply entering a mobile number does not necessarily equal compliant opt-in, and opt-outs must be acknowledged and acted on.

Source: [Twilio U.S. SMS Compliance Guide](https://www.twilio.com/en-us/resource-center/guide-to-us-sms-compliance)

The FTC’s Do Not Call guidance says the National DNC Registry applies to sales calls, including mobile numbers. Companies can call if the consumer gave written permission or has a recent business relationship, but must stop if asked. Robocalls selling something require written permission directly from the consumer.

Source: [FTC National Do Not Call Registry FAQ](https://consumer.ftc.gov/national-do-not-call-registry-faqs)

**Operator rule:** use SMS only after the seller opts in, texts/calls you first, fills a form with consent language, or you have counsel-approved consent. SMS is best for inbound speed-to-lead and appointment follow-up, not cold blasting.

## Direct Mail

Direct mail is the safest cold channel of the three, but do not use deceptive formats. No fake “official notice,” fake government language, fake foreclosure rescue language, or fake checks unless your counsel has blessed it.

Direct mail can be tracked with QR codes, PURLs, matchbacks, unique phone numbers, business reply cards, and campaign-specific landing pages. USPS specifically recommends QR codes, PURLs, vanity URLs, matchbacks, and other tools for attribution.

Source: [USPS Direct Mail ROI Tracking](https://www.uspsdelivers.com/track-direct-mail-roi/)

## Foreclosure and Distress Language

Be careful with homeowners in foreclosure. The FTC’s Mortgage Assistance Relief Services guidance says businesses offering mortgage assistance relief cannot charge upfront fees, must make key disclosures, cannot tell people to stop paying or stop communicating with lenders, and cannot misrepresent services. It also notes the rule can cover real estate agents who promote services as a way to help consumers avoid foreclosure, while ordinary buying/selling services are treated differently.

Source: [FTC Mortgage Assistance Relief Services Rule Compliance Guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/mortgage-assistance-relief-services-rule-compliance-guide-business)

**Operator rule:** you are not “saving them from foreclosure.” You are making an as-is purchase offer. Do not give mortgage/legal advice. Do not say “we can stop foreclosure” unless counsel has approved the exact process and disclosures.

# 2. Channel Strategy

## Cold Email’s Job

Cold email should do four things:

1. Verify interest quickly.
2. Start low-friction conversations.
3. Test messaging cheaply before spending on mail.
4. Support direct mail by creating familiarity.

Cold email is not where you close the deal. It is where you get a seller to say:

> “Maybe. What would you offer?”

## Direct Mail’s Job

Direct mail is your trust layer.

In real estate investing, direct mail response rates are often cited around **0.5% to 2%**, sometimes higher in smaller or less competitive markets, but the real metric is cost per deal, not response rate. One REI marketing source cites $2,500–$3,000 cost per direct-mail deal as effective in an average-sized market, with higher costs in more competitive markets.

Source: [Ballpoint Marketing — Direct Marketing Costs for Real Estate Investors](https://ballpointmarketing.com/blogs/investing/direct-marketing-costs-real-estate-investors?srsltid=AfmBOoqxI7amSxVIcqzIHxxbYHgJnLA3PpGx9p8NToM6IYMt6DMOfxd_)

USPS also reports modern direct mail can be integrated with QR/PURL tracking and cites direct mail ROI benchmarks of 56% for house lists and 67% for prospect lists from the 2025 ANA Response Rate Report.

Source: [USPS Data-Driven Direct Mail](https://www.uspsdelivers.com/data-driven-direct-mail/)

## SMS’s Job

SMS should handle:

- Speed-to-lead after inbound form submission.
- Appointment confirmation.
- Follow-up after a real conversation.
- “Can I send you the offer link?” after permission.

Do not make SMS your cold weapon. That is how operators turn a marketing campaign into a legal expense line item.

# 3. List Strategy for Distressed Real Estate

Your list is the campaign. Copy helps, cadence helps, tools help, but bad data kills everything.

## Core Seller Segments

Build separate campaigns for each list type. Do not mix them all into one generic “we buy houses” blast.

### High-Equity Absentee Owners

Owners who do not live at the property and likely have room to sell below retail while still walking away with cash.

### Vacant Properties

Strong for direct mail and property-address-based email. Use neutral language. Do not say, “I noticed your house is abandoned.” Say:

> “I’m reaching out about {{property_address}}.”

### Probate / Inherited

High sensitivity. Use softer language. Avoid “probate” in subject lines unless you want to sound like a courthouse vulture.

### Tax Delinquent

Good signal, but do not say “you are behind on taxes” in email. That feels invasive.

### Code Violations / Liens

Useful signal. Again, do not weaponize it in the first touch.

### Tired Landlords

Absentee + multiple years owned + tenant/eviction indicators. Message around tenants, repairs, and property management fatigue.

### Pre-Foreclosure

Highest sensitivity and legal risk. Keep language clean: “as-is offer,” “flexible closing,” “no pressure.” No rescue claims.

### Failed Listings / Expireds

Message around “no listing, no showings, no repairs.”

## Lead Fields You Need in Your CRM/List

At minimum:

- Owner first name
- Owner last name
- Owner mailing address
- Property address
- Property city/state/ZIP
- Email
- Phone
- Absentee status
- Equity estimate
- Ownership length
- Mortgage/lien indicator
- Tax delinquency flag
- Vacancy flag
- Probate/inherited flag
- Mail round
- Mail drop date
- Direct-mail creative ID
- Email campaign ID
- Last contact date
- Last response
- Lead status
- Do-not-contact flag

Use one master record per property/owner. Do not let email, mail, and phone vendors create separate silos. That is how you accidentally keep hitting someone who already told you to stop.

# 4. Cold Email Infrastructure

## Domain Setup

Do not send cold outreach from your main company domain. Use separate but legitimate outreach domains.

Example:

```text
Main brand: martinhomebuyers.com
Outreach domains: martinoffers.com, martinpropertybuyers.com, martinhomeoffers.com
```

Do not use deceptive domains. The domain should clearly connect to your business.

## DNS Requirements

Each sending domain needs:

- MX
- SPF
- DKIM
- DMARC
- Domain forwarding
- Valid inboxes
- Business signature
- Real reply handling

Instantly’s DNS documentation says MX, SPF, DKIM, and DMARC work together for deliverability and reply handling. It specifically says all four are needed for optimal email functionality and deliverability.

Source: [Instantly — MX, SPF, DKIM, DMARC, Domain Forwarding](https://help.instantly.ai/en/articles/6222192-how-to-set-up-mx-spf-dkim-dmarc-domain-forwarding)

## Warmup and Volume

Instantly recommends warming up existing accounts for at least two weeks before launching campaigns and says to keep warmup enabled. Their account/campaign limit documentation explains that the recommended account limit is **30 campaign emails per day per account**, and campaign limits should be calculated as number of accounts × 30.

Source: [Instantly — Cold Email Strategy](https://help.instantly.ai/en/articles/5975326-instantly-cold-email-strategy)

Instantly also recommends 30 campaign emails + 10 warmup emails per email account per day, and its campaign slow-ramp system starts at 2 campaign emails on day 1 and increases by 2 per day until reaching the max, hitting 30/day on day 15.

Source: [Instantly — Campaign Options](https://help.instantly.ai/en/articles/6222396-campaign-options)

## Starter Setup

```text
3 domains
2 inboxes per domain
6 inboxes total
Slow ramp on
Max 30 campaign emails/inbox/day
Max full volume: 180 campaign emails/day
Initial max new leads: 50–75/day because follow-ups will consume volume
```

Scale by adding more domains/inboxes, not by hammering the same inbox harder.

# 6. Cold Email Cadence

The right cadence is not “7 aggressive follow-ups because some SaaS bro said persistence wins.” Homeowners are not VP Sales prospects. They are private individuals with a valuable asset. Push too hard and you become noise.

Instantly says most cold outreach campaigns should use a maximum of 3–4 steps. Belkins’ 2025 study of 16.5 million cold emails found average reply rates around 5.8%, best performance from 6–8 sentence emails under 200 words, and noted that reply rates decline with more follow-ups. A separate Belkins follow-up study says 4+ emails more than triples unsubscribe and spam complaint rates. Instantly’s 2026 benchmark report, meanwhile, argues 4–7 touchpoints can work when each follow-up adds genuine new value.

Sources:

- [Instantly — Campaign Setup Guide](https://help.instantly.ai/en/articles/6386134-how-to-create-a-campaign-complete-setup-guide)
- [Belkins — Sales Follow-Up Statistics](https://belkins.io/blog/sales-follow-up-statistics)
- [Instantly — Cold Email Benchmark Report 2026](https://instantly.ai/cold-email-benchmark-report-2026)

Treat that as the nuance:

> **Short active sequence, then shift to nurture.**

## Pure Cold Email Intro Sequence

| Day | Step | Purpose |
|---:|---|---|
| Day 1 | Email 1 | Property-specific intro |
| Day 4 | Email 2 | Different angle, not “checking in” |
| Day 8 | Email 3 | As-is/convenience angle |
| Day 14 | Email 4 | Close-the-loop |

Then move non-responders to 30–45 day nurture.

## Direct-Mail-Supported Email Sequence

| Day | Step |
|---:|---|
| Day 0 | Mail drop |
| Day 3–7 | Expected mail arrival window |
| Day 5 | Email 1 referencing the letter |
| Day 9 | Email 2 |
| Day 15 | Email 3 |
| Day 21–30 | Second mail piece |
| Day 25 | Email 4 |
| Day 45 | Nurture begins |

USPS Informed Delivery can give business mailers an integrated physical/digital touchpoint and provides digital previews, ride-along content, extra impressions, and campaign insights. If your mail vendor supports it, use it.

Source: [USPS Informed Delivery for Business](https://www.usps.com/business/informed-delivery.htm)

# 8. Subject Line Rules

Subject lines must be accurate. CAN-SPAM prohibits deceptive subject lines, and Gmail/Yahoo punish behavior that creates complaints.

Source: [FTC CAN-SPAM Compliance Guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business)

## Use Boring, Specific Subject Lines

```text
{{property_address}}
note about {{property_address}}
quick question
as-is offer
{{property_city}} property
should I close this?
your property on {{street_name}}
```

## Avoid

```text
URGENT
FINAL NOTICE
RE: closing documents
Official property notice
Cash approved
You qualify
Foreclosure help
Government program
I have your buyer
```

Fake urgency gets opens and burns trust.

# 9. Direct Mail Strategy

## Best Formats

### 1. Personal Letter

Best for high-value/high-intent lists: pre-foreclosure, probate, vacant, tax delinquent, tired landlord.

Use a real envelope. Keep the letter one page. Make it specific to the property.

### 2. Postcard

Best for volume and repeated touches. The CTA is visible without opening. Use for absentee/high-equity/vacant campaigns.

### 3. Credibility Mailer

Send after the first touch. Include who you are, what you buy, how the process works, and proof you are real.

### 4. Handwritten-Style Letter

Can work, but do not make it feel fake. If it looks like a gimmick, it backfires.

### 5. Check Mailer

High response potential, high skepticism. Use cautiously. I would not lead with it unless you know your market tolerates it.

## Direct Mail Copy

### Postcard Front

```text
Would you consider selling {{property_address}} as-is?

No repairs. No showings. No agent fees. Flexible closing.
```

### Postcard Back

```text
Hi {{first_name}},

I’m interested in buying {{property_address}} directly from you.

If you have thought about selling, we can make the process simple: no repairs, no cleanup, no open houses, and no public listing.

Call or text {{phone}}
Or visit {{purl_or_landing_page}}

If you are not interested, no problem.

— {{your_name}}
{{company}}
{{business_address}}
```

### Letter

```text
Hi {{first_name}},

I’m writing about {{property_address}}.

My company buys properties in {{property_city}} directly from owners. We usually work with people who want a simpler sale than listing with an agent: no repairs, no showings, no cleanup, and no long waiting period.

I do not know your situation, and I do not want to assume anything. I simply wanted to ask whether you would consider an as-is offer.

If so, call or text me at {{phone}}. You can also visit {{landing_page}}.

If you are not interested, no problem.

Thank you,
{{your_name}}
{{company}}
{{business_address}}
```

# 10. How Cold Email and Direct Mail Work Together

## The Right Sequence

Direct mail creates credibility. Email creates speed. Use both around the same property record.

### 45-Day Acquisition Sprint

| Day | Action |
|---:|---|
| Day 0 | Send letter/postcard |
| Day 5 | Email referencing letter |
| Day 9 | Email with as-is angle |
| Day 15 | Email with convenience angle |
| Day 21 | Second mail piece |
| Day 25 | Close-the-loop email |
| Day 35 | Third mail piece to non-responders |
| Day 45 | Move to monthly nurture |

## The Psychology

A seller gets your letter. Maybe they ignore it.

Then they see a short email mentioning the same property. Now you are not random. You are familiar.

Then they get another postcard. Now you look persistent but not chaotic.

The key is consistency, not harassment.

## Tracking

Every mail piece should use at least two attribution tools:

- Unique phone number by campaign or segment
- PURL or landing page
- QR code with UTM
- Mail piece ID
- CRM matchback
- Call recording/transcription
- Inbound form source
- Email campaign ID

USPS specifically lists QR codes, PURLs, vanity URLs, matchbacks, promo codes, and reply cards as ways to track direct mail ROI. USPS also notes that QR/PURL tracking can show scans, location, operating system, and what users did after scanning.

Source: [USPS Direct Mail ROI Tracking](https://www.uspsdelivers.com/track-direct-mail-roi/)

# 11. Landing Page Structure

Your landing page should not look like a national “we buy houses” template from 2011.

Use:

```text
Headline: Sell {{city}} property as-is without repairs or showings
Subhead: Get a direct offer. No obligation. No agent fees. Flexible closing.
```

Required fields:

- Name
- Property address
- Phone
- Email
- Timeline
- Property condition
- Consent checkbox for SMS/email follow-up
- Privacy note
- Business name/address
- Real phone number
- Simple FAQ
- Proof: testimonials, local purchases, BBB/Google reviews if real

Do not put ten paragraphs about “we are passionate about helping homeowners.” Nobody believes that. Tell them what happens next.

# 12. Long Nurture Campaigns

Most owners are not ready now. That does not mean they are bad leads. It means your campaign needs memory.

## Nurture Timing

After intro sequence:

```text
30 days
60 days
90 days
120 days
180 days
Quarterly after that
```

For direct mail:

```text
Monthly for first 3–4 months on high-value segments
Every 60–90 days after that
Stop after clear opt-out, sale, bad data, or no longer target-fit
```

## Nurture Email Topics

| Month | Topic |

# 13. Objection Handling

## “Not interested.”

```text
Understood. I’ll stop following up.

If anything changes later and you want an as-is number for {{property_address}}, you can reach me at {{phone}}.

— {{your_name}}
```

Then suppress them. Do not “just one more thing” them.

## “How did you get my information?”

```text
I found the property through public property records and used a data provider to locate contact information. I can remove your information from my list now.

Would you like me to do that?
```

## “What’s your offer?”

Do not throw a blind number unless your strategy is to insult people at scale.

```text
I can give you a range, but I need a few basics first so I’m not guessing.

Is the property occupied or vacant?
Any major repairs needed?
If you sold, what timeline would you want?

After that I can tell you whether we are even in the ballpark.
```

## “You’re trying to steal my house.”

```text
I understand why it can feel that way. A direct as-is offer is usually lower than a perfect retail listing because we take on repairs, holding costs, resale risk, and closing certainty.

If listing with an agent gets you a better outcome, you should do that. My offer only makes sense if speed, convenience, privacy, or avoiding repairs matters more than getting the absolute highest retail price.

Would you still want a number to compare?
```

## “I want Zillow price.”

```text
That may be possible if the property is fully repaired, listed publicly, and a retail buyer can get financing.

Our offer would be an as-is investor offer, so it will not match a retail/Zillow number. The tradeoff is no repairs, no showings, no commissions, and a flexible close.

Do you want the as-is number anyway, or would retail listing be your better route?
```

## “Are you a wholesaler?”

Do not lie.

```text
We buy directly and also work with funding/partner buyers depending on the deal. If an agreement involves an assignment or partner purchase, that will be disclosed in the paperwork. No surprises.

Are you mainly concerned about certainty of closing?
```

## “Take me off your list.”

```text
Done. I’ll remove you from future contact.
```

Then actually suppress them. Immediately.

# 14. Testing and Optimization

## Do Not Test Everything at Once

Test one major variable at a time:

- List segment
- Subject line
- First sentence
- CTA
- Direct mail format
- Direct mail headline
- Timing
- Offer framing
- Direct mail + email vs email-only
- Postcard vs letter

Outreach recommends reviewing sequence performance, A/B testing emails, and not deleting underperforming variants because you lose the data. It also says each email should be delivered to at least 250 addresses before you treat the data as reliable.

Source: [Outreach — Sequence Review Best Practices](https://support.outreach.io/support/solutions/articles/159000426250-best-practices-for-reviewing-your-outreach-sequences)

## Email KPIs

Track:

- Delivery rate
- Bounce rate
- Spam complaint rate
- Positive reply rate
- Negative reply rate
- Neutral reply rate
- Appointment rate
- Offer request rate
- Contract rate
- Close rate
- Revenue per 1,000 records
- Cost per qualified seller lead
- Cost per contract
- Cost per closed deal

Open rate is secondary. Tracking pixels can hurt deliverability, and Apple/privacy changes have made opens less reliable. Belkins says they stopped tracking opens mid-year because tracking pixels were hurting deliverability.

Source: [Belkins — Sales Follow-Up Statistics](https://belkins.io/blog/sales-follow-up-statistics)

## Direct Mail KPIs

Track:

- Response rate
- Call rate
- Text/form rate
- Qualified lead rate
- Cost per lead
- Cost per appointment
- Cost per offer
- Cost per contract
- Cost per closed deal
- Average spread/profit
- Revenue per mail piece
- List segment ROI
- Creative ROI
- Mail round ROI

## Testing Matrix

| Test | Control | Variant | Success Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject line | `{{property_address}}` | `quick question` | Positive reply rate |
| CTA | “Open to an offer?” | “Should I close the file?” | Positive reply rate |
| Mail type | Postcard | Letter | Cost per qualified lead |
| Timing | Email before mail | Email after mail | Lead rate |
| Segment | Absentee high equity | Vacant high equity | Cost per contract |
| Offer framing | Cash/as-is | Flexible close/no repairs | Appointment rate |

# 15. Conversion Expectations and Funnel Math

Use this as a planning model, not a promise.

## For 10,000 Property Records — Cold Email Model

```text
Email found/verified: maybe 2,000–4,000 depending on data quality
Delivered: 90–98% if verified
Positive replies: 1–5% depending on segment and copy
Qualified seller leads: 20–40% of positive replies
Appointments/serious calls: 30–60% of qualified leads
Offers made: 50–90% of serious calls
Contracts: 5–25% of offers
Closes: 50–85% of signed contracts depending on title, seller quality, and acquisition discipline
```

## For 10,000 Direct Mail Pieces

```text
REI direct mail response: often 0.5–2%
Responses: 50–200
Qualified seller leads: 15–60
Contracts: 1–10 depending on market/list/pricing
Closed deals: 1–7 depending on title, funding, seller motivation, and competition
```

The tactical point: do not worship response rate. A probate/vacant/tax-delinquent campaign with a low response rate can outperform a broad absentee-owner campaign if the callers are more motivated and the purchase prices are better.

---

# 16. Acquisition Call Flow

Once someone replies, your marketing job ends and acquisitions begins.

## First Call Structure

1. Confirm property.
2. Build trust.
3. Ask why they responded.
4. Ask condition.
5. Ask occupancy.
6. Ask timeline.
7. Ask mortgage/liens/taxes if appropriate.
8. Ask price expectation.
9. Explain your process.
10. Set next step.

## Seller Qualification Questions

```text
Are you living in the property, renting it, or is it vacant?

What made you consider selling?

What repairs would you handle before listing it?

If you did sell, when would you ideally want to close?

Do you have a number in mind?

Is anyone else involved in the decision?

Are there any liens, back taxes, mortgage payments, or title issues we should know about?

Would you prefer the highest possible price through listing, or a simpler as-is sale?
```

The last question is money. It frames the tradeoff cleanly.

---

# 17. Offer Positioning

Do not pretend your cash offer is retail. Say the quiet part professionally.

A direct buyer offer is lower because you are giving:

- Speed
- Certainty
- No repairs
- No showings
- No agent commissions
- No financing contingency
- Flexible move-out
- Privacy
- Problem-solving

Use this comparison:

| Option | Tradeoff |
|---|---|
| Retail listing | Highest possible price, slower, repairs/showings/commissions/uncertainty |
| Direct as-is sale | Lower price, faster, simpler, more certain |

That framing reduces hostility because you are not insulting their intelligence.

# 20. Suppression and Reputation Management

Create a global suppression file:

- Unsubscribed
- Do not call
- Do not text
- Threatened legal complaint
- Hostile reply
- Wrong person
- Sold property
- Agent-only
- Deceased/no proper contact
- Bad email
- Bad phone
- Returned mail
- Already in negotiation
- Closed deal
- Competitor/skip

Every campaign must check the suppression list before sending.

Google says spam rates should stay below 0.1% and never reach 0.3% or higher; Yahoo also requires spam rates below 0.3%. Treat 0.1% as the danger line and 0.3% as “you are actively damaging infrastructure.”

# 21. The Actual Campaign I Would Run First

## Pilot Market

Pick one county/metro. Do not go national immediately. National scale with broken systems just creates national chaos.

## List

Start with 5,000–10,000 records:

- Absentee owners
- High equity
- Owned 7+ years
- Single-family/duplex
- Out-of-state or out-of-county owner
- Layer with vacancy/tax/code/probate where possible
- Remove recent sales
- Remove corporate owners unless targeting landlords
- Remove protected/sensitive categories you cannot handle ethically/compliantly

## Direct Mail

Send 2,500–5,000 pieces first, not 50,000.

| Round | Timing | Mail Piece |
|---:|---|---|
| Round 1 | Day 0 | Personal letter |
| Round 2 | 30 days later | Postcard |
| Round 3 | 60 days later | Credibility/process mailer |
| Round 4 | 90 days later | Final/simple offer postcard |

## Email

Run only verified emails.

- 4-step sequence over 14 days.
- Reference the property.
- Reference mail when applicable.
- No links in first touch.
- No open tracking at first.
- Stop on reply.
- Daily limits controlled.

## SMS

Only for inbound/opt-in:

- Immediate response after form fill
- Appointment reminders
- Offer follow-up
- “Can I text you the offer summary?” after permission

## Success Threshold

After first 60–90 days, judge by:

- Cost per qualified seller lead
- Cost per signed contract
- Contract-to-close rate
- Average deal spread
- Seller lead quality
- Reply sentiment
- Deliverability health

Not by “open rate.” Opens are vanity. Contracts are reality.

# 22. Final Tactical Notes

The seller-facing message should never be:

> “I want to buy your distressed property at a massive discount.”

The message is:

> “I can give you a simple as-is option if convenience, speed, or certainty matters more than listing retail.”

The internal machine should be ruthless:

```text
Bad list? Kill it.
Bad segment? Kill it.
Bad copy? Rewrite it.
High negative replies? Pause it.
High bounces? Stop sending.
Seller says no? Suppress them.
Seller shows motivation? Call immediately.
Lead says “maybe later”? Nurture forever until sold, dead, or removed.
```

The winners in this niche are not the ones with the cleverest subject line. They are the ones with cleaner data, faster follow-up, better seller conversations, disciplined underwriting, and enough persistence to keep touching the right owners without acting like a spam goblin.
